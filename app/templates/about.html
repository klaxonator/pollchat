TL;DR

  • Spot Twitter trends across competitive electoral districts
  • Who’s twittering about the 2018 U.S. elections, and how often?
  • What hashtags are trending where?
  • What issues are being discussed (or imposed) in multiple districts?

WHAT’S THE POINT?

PollChatter is driven by a simple idea: that we, as voters and participants in a democratic society, should be able to understand who is trying to influence us, and why.

We can’t tell you who people really are on Twitter. Nor do we want to. There ought to be some privacy in this world.

But we can show you trends. Across districts, across screen names, across hashtags. If anyone’s trying to move the political needle (and we all know they are), there’s a chance it might show up here.

So: click around and see what’s happening. And then go out and shake the world up.


WHO ARE YOU?

Pollchatter is primarily the work of John Borland, an award-winning former political and technology reporter (Cnet News.com, Wired News, California Journal, etc.) currently engaged in data journalism and digital activism, alongside other activities. A number of other people both on and offline have offered valuable suggestions and critiques. It is the first public entry in the broader Mayfly Research project.


WHOSE SIDE ARE YOU ON?

We’re on the side of transparency. We're not associated with any political party, but that certainly doesn't mean we don't have opinions.

Kenzaburo Oe, the Japanese Nobel Prize-winning novelist, once described himself as “an anarchist who loves democracy.” We think this idea ought to be taken seriously.


WHAT’S THE LONG GAME?

We live in a big-data society. It is very clearly not an information democracy. So what to do?

Fight data with data, is our idea. It’s probably not good physics, but until someone has a better idea, we’ll keep coding.
